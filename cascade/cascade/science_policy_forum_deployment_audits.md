# Audit deployment architectures for military AI

Frontier AI models are entering intelligence analysis, battlefield planning, cyber defense, logistics, surveillance, and human-machine command. As they do, military AI governance has fixed on the wrong unit of certification. It asks whether a model passed safety evaluations. It should ask who can alter the model's constraints after the test is over.

The distinction is not rhetorical. A model test certifies a configuration, not a deployment. The configuration that passes evaluation (a particular model version, behind a particular system prompt, with a fixed retrieval source, a defined set of tool permissions, a bounded autonomy envelope, and a known waiver authority) can be changed the day after approval, by actors the evaluation never examined. Each of those components is a control surface, and each can be modified by a contractor, an integrator, a vendor, an agency, or a command. When any of them changes, the certificate the test issued no longer describes the system in the field.

The policy consequence is a single reframing: military AI should be certified not as a model alone, but as a changeable deployment architecture. Certification must cover not only model behavior under test but post-test change authority: who holds the power to alter the constraint architecture, and what happens to the certificate when they use it.

## Why model tests fall short

Model evaluations are necessary and not sufficient. Red-team exercises, benchmark suites, usage policies, and output audits can reveal whether a model generates prohibited content, assists a dangerous task, or refuses legitimate requests. They cannot, by themselves, identify which part of a deployed system made a harmful outcome possible, because a high-risk deployment is not a model in isolation. It is a control system: a model wrapped in a system prompt, connected to retrieval and tools, fed sensor data, routed through an interface, filtered by classifiers, monitored by humans, and governed by an authority that can change any of those components. The same base model can pass a laboratory evaluation and behave differently once integrated into a classified workflow, attached to operational data, or placed under a different authority.

Output review sees what crossed the interface, not the architecture that produced it. A harmful recommendation could originate in a user prompt, a brittle interface rule, a contaminated training distribution, a tool-integration error, or an authorized override from above. A benign output can conceal a dangerous topology if the system can be reconfigured after approval. This is why output-only audits are weakest exactly where military AI is most consequential: the visible trace is reviewed while the decisive changes sit in hidden configuration, versioning, tool access, and authority.

Systems safety learned this long ago. In Nancy Leveson's systems-theoretic process analysis (STPA), accidents emerge from mismatches among control actions, feedback, and constraints in a control structure, not merely from defective components (5). Algorithmic-fairness research reached a parallel conclusion in the "Ripple Effect Trap": inserting an algorithm into a social system changes incentives and feedback beyond the technical object (6). The missing instrument for military AI is a way to turn those insights into deployment audits, organized around the boundary that governs what a reviewer can actually recover.

A recent dispute illustrates the gap and opens the policy window; it does not have to carry the argument. Public announcements in 2024 described Anthropic, Palantir, and Amazon Web Services working to make Claude models available to U.S. defense and intelligence agencies (1), and Anthropic later introduced Claude Gov models for classified customers (2). In February 2026, Associated Press reporting described Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth pressing Anthropic for broader military use while the company resisted certain use cases (3); other reporting tied the dispute to the Maven environment (4). Even if the record narrows, the policy lesson survives the details: safety is not settled when a model passes a test, because the authority to change the deployed system outlives the test.

## A cascade of constraints

To audit a deployment, one must name its parts. The Constraint Cascade partitions a deployed AI system into three operational layers and a fourth that governs them (Fig. 1).

Layer 1, the Environment, is the operating environment: mission setting, data distribution, sensor feeds, adversary behavior, coalition data, and the operators who frame requests. Layer 2, the Interface, is the deployment interface: system prompt, retrieval pipeline, tool permissions, API parameters, autonomy envelope, memory state, policy filters, escalation rules, human-review points, and logging. L2 is where a model becomes an operational system; with adequate logs and topology disclosure it is reconstructible, though in classified, vendor-integrated systems even L2 can be opaque. Layer 3, Algorithm & Weights, is the model substrate: base model, fine-tuning, reinforcement learning, reward models, training-data composition, and learned representations, often proprietary and inaccessible to an agency that sees only outputs and configuration.

Layer 4, Strategic Authority, is the control plane of the cascade. It is not the top of a stack but the meta-authority layer whose distinctive power is to rewrite the constraints governing L1 through L3 after certification: to set deployment goals, allocate safety resources, approve exceptions, change vendors, compel access, modify contracts, and waive restrictions. L4 can be corporate (product strategy, safety resourcing, liability avoidance) or sovereign (procurement mandates, national-security overrides, regulatory compulsion), and it can be split between them: the conflict between a corporate decision to keep a constraint and a sovereign demand to remove it is itself a diagnostic signal.

The most important audit boundary lies between L2 and L3. It is not an absolute inspectability divide but a reconstructibility divide: with adequate logs, reviewers can recover inputs, prompts, interface settings, tool calls, and operator actions; below the boundary they confront weights, activations, and training distributions that outputs alone cannot reveal. This does not make auditing impossible. It fixes what must be audited, not only the output but the deployment topology and the control plane that can change it. Model tests evaluate one layer of this architecture; deployment audits ask how the layers interact, and who may rewire them.

## Three failure patterns

Different failures look identical at the output layer. Three cross-layer patterns matter most for military AI, and each implies a different remedy.

*Override* is a conflict of authority. When a higher layer's decision overrides a lower layer's safety constraint, the higher layer wins, not because the constraint malfunctioned, but because it was structurally subordinate. The Anthropic dispute illustrates an authority-conflict pattern, not a completed override: the company's stated position is that it resisted two categories, mass domestic surveillance of Americans and fully autonomous weapons (3, 7), while AP reporting describes officials weighing a supply-chain-risk designation or Defense Production Act authority if access were not broadened (3). The diagnostic question is not whether one answer was safe. It is whether strategic change authority could alter the constraint envelope after safety review. If a system is approved with one set of guardrails and a later authority can remove them, the original test no longer describes the deployed system.

*Contamination* has two forms that must not be conflated. Context contamination occurs when sensor feeds, retrieval documents, mission files, or adversarial inputs distort the L2 context without changing weights; it is reversible through source isolation and provenance controls. Adaptation contamination occurs when operational signals cross the L2/L3 boundary into the substrate (through fine-tuning, reinforcement learning on operational logs, continual learning, or adapter updates) and calls instead for training-loop controls and model recertification. The distinction makes the pathway into the substrate precise and answers the obvious objection: a corrupted retrieval source is not a corrupted model, and the two demand different fixes.

*Compression* is the pattern most particular to layered safety, and the easiest to misdiagnose as good caution. When multiple reasonable constraints activate at once (L1 sensitivity labels, L2 access controls, L3 refusal training, L4 classification or liability rules), their intersection can collapse to near-zero. An analyst asking for the basis of a target recommendation may receive silence, generic caveats, or an unusable summary at the moment human judgment most needs a clear explanation. Symptoms include refusal spikes, explanation collapse, uncertainty omission, escalation loops, and inconsistent access denials. Compression is not over-safety; it is unmanaged constraint interaction. It is a system-level failure in which individually defensible guardrails collide with no architecture for resolving the conflict.

These patterns point to different remedies. Override requires authority controls; context contamination requires source isolation and provenance; adaptation contamination requires training-loop controls; compression requires conflict-resolution design. An evaluation that records only the final answer cannot tell them apart.

## Three audit artifacts

Three artifacts turn the Cascade from a vocabulary into an auditable mechanism, and one cross-cutting rule binds them.

First, *deployment topology disclosure.* The deploying agency, with contractual obligations flowing to model providers, cloud providers, prime contractors, and integrators, should be able to show a cleared auditor (an inspector general, acquisition authority, or accredited review body) the system map: model family and version; interface wrapper; system prompts at an appropriate level of abstraction; retrieval sources; tool permissions; autonomy limits; human-review points; logging coverage; fallback modes; update cadence; and the party with authority over each component. Disclosure need not expose trade secrets or classified detail to the public. It is the wiring diagram one demands before certifying an aircraft control system: it is not enough that a component passed bench testing; reviewers must know where it sits in the control loop. Model evaluations cannot substitute, because they test behavior under one configuration. Existing evaluation and documentation practices, including HELM, sociotechnical safety evaluation, model cards, and datasheets, specify neither the deployed topology nor who may change it after review (10–13). Anthropic's own Claude Gov announcement notes reduced refusal when engaging classified material (2), exactly the kind of interface-and-context change that must be visible to an auditor.

Second, *constraint-authority logs.* These record who can modify guardrails, system prompts, retrieval sources, tool permissions, model versions, autonomy settings, escalation thresholds, exception policies, and waivers. They also record when each change is made, under what authority, and with what justification. Responsibility sits with whoever holds change authority: providers for model and policy changes, integrators for interface and tools, platforms for access controls, government customers for mission exceptions and operational waivers. In military contexts these logs should be built for classified environments from the start. The point is not real-time transparency but institutional memory: after an incident, reviewers should not have to reconstruct governance from emails and recollection.

Third, *layer-specific incident review.* When a system contributes to a harmful or near-harmful event, review should not stop at whether the output was wrong. It should assign the failure to one or more Cascade layers and name the cross-layer pattern. An L1 review asks whether the environment, data, or operator inputs created a hazard the system was not built for; L2, whether the interface, tools, or display mediated it incorrectly; L3, whether weights or training produced behavior the interface could not correct; L4, whether the control plane changed the safety envelope: under-resourcing a known fix, approving an exception, mandating broader access, or shifting the objective. The same uncertainty-free targeting summary might come from bad sensor data (L1), a display choice (L2), a calibration defect (L3), or an instruction to compress uncertainty for speed (L4); each implies a different fix. Such reviews require bodies with access to logs, topology disclosures, and change histories (defense acquisition authorities, inspectors general, safety centers, AI-assurance units), and protocols interoperable across coalition allies.

Binding the three is a single rule: *recertification triggers.* A material change to model version, system prompt, retrieval corpus, tool permissions, autonomy envelope, or waiver authority voids the prior certificate and requires re-audit or, at minimum, a recertification memorandum stating the change, its authority, and its justification. Triggers can be tiered by change severity, so routine reconfiguration does not halt operational tempo while material changes (a new model version, a relaxed safety filter, a broadened autonomy envelope) re-open the certificate. This is what makes the mechanism executable. A model can pass review on Monday and become part of a different system by Friday if an authorized actor swaps the version, relaxes a filter, adds a tool, or grants an exception; the recertification trigger is the institutional answer to the question traditional evaluation cannot pose: who had the power to do that, and did the certificate follow.

## Auditing procurement and incidents

Military AI policy already senses that "human control" is not self-executing. U.S. policy on autonomy in weapon systems emphasizes appropriate human judgment over the use of force, and international declarations on responsible military AI stress testing, assurance, and accountability (8, 9). But human judgment is not a property of a model; it is a property of a deployed control architecture. A human cannot meaningfully supervise a model-mediated workflow if the interface hides uncertainty, the retrieval system silently changes context, the weights encode unexamined adaptation, or the control plane can waive constraints without traceable review.

Deployment audits should therefore become a condition of high-risk military-AI procurement. Not every chatbot needs this. But systems used for intelligence analysis, targeting support, cyber operations, surveillance, autonomous-platform coordination, or escalation-relevant decision support should not be certified by model behavior under test alone. They should be certified as deployed systems, with documented topology, logged change authority, recertification triggers, and layer-specific incident review.

The governing question is not only who decides the rules, but where each rule lives and who may move it: which rules are in the model, which in the interface, which depend on the mission environment, and which can be changed by a contractor, commander, agency, or vendor after approval; and when something fails, whether reviewers can tell which layer failed. A model test certifies a configuration. Safety in war requires certifying the architecture that can change it.

A schematic shows three operational layers and one control plane: L1 Environment (operating environment), L2 Interface (deployment interface), L3 Algorithm & Weights (model substrate), and L4 Strategic Authority (the control plane / change-authority layer). A dashed line between L2 and L3 marks the reconstructibility boundary: output-only review sees the trace but not the hidden model state. Arrows show Override pressure from L4 to L2/L3, context contamination from L1 into L2, adaptation contamination from L1/L2 across the boundary into L3, and Compression acting across all layers.
